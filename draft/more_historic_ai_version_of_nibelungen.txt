A Historically Reimagined Nibelungenlied

This version is set between roughly 425 and 437 AD in Roman Gaul. The Western Roman Empire is crumbling, and its general, Flavius Aetius, masterfully plays Germanic tribes against each other to maintain a semblance of control.
Principal Characters (Proto-Germanic Names)
Sigifriþuz: (Siegfried) – The charismatic and wildly successful warlord of the Salian Franks, a tribe known for raiding along the lower Rhine. Not a prince, but a "new money" warrior who has accumulated immense wealth.
Grimhildiz: (Kriemhild) – Sister of Gunþiharjaz, a noble Burgundian woman known for her pride and fierce family loyalty.
Gunþiharjaz: (Gunther) – King of the Burgundian foederati, settled by Rome around the city of Vangionum (Worms). A political strategist, but not a great warrior, anxious about his kingdom's precarious position.
Brunihildiz: (Brünhild) – The warrior-princess and military leader of a rival Alamannic tribe. A political prize whose allegiance can secure the entire upper Rhine frontier.
Haganaz: (Hagen) – Gunþiharjaz’s most trusted commander (comes), a grim, pragmatic, and utterly loyal veteran of the Rhine border wars. He is the ultimate guardian of the Burgundian kingdom's security.
Flavius Aetius: The Roman Magister Militum of Gaul. The story's ultimate power broker, who views all Germanic tribes as temporary assets or liabilities. He is the stand-in for "Etzel" (Attila), not as a husband, but as a political manipulator and the ultimate arbiter of destruction.

Part One: The Rise of Ambition, the Seeds of Ruin

The story begins around 430 AD. Sigifriþuz, leading his Salian Frankish warband, has become legend. His riches, looted from Roman settlements and trade routes in Belgica, are known as the "Rhinegold". His fame is not magical; he is a brilliant guerrilla tactician who has never been defeated in open battle, leading his men to believe the gods favor him. His "invulnerability" is a reputation that precedes him.

At Vangionum, the Burgundian King Gunþiharjaz watches this upstart Frank with a mix of fear and opportunity. Gunþiharjaz needs to secure his northern-eastern border and solidify his own power against the presure of other tribes. He sets his sights on a strategic alliance with the Alamanni through a marriage to their formidable leader, Brunihildiz. However, Brunihildiz has sworn she will only ally with a leader who can prove his military might by capturing the Roman fort of Noviomagus Nemetum (Speyer), which is held by the Vindices (according to notitia dignitatum, an auxilium palatinum unit).

Gunþiharjaz is a diplomat, not a conqueror. He invites Sigifriþuz to his court with a proposition: if Sigifriþuz's Franks can lead the assault on Noviomagus under the Burgundian banner, making Gunþiharjaz the victor, then Sigifriþuz will have the hand of his renowned sister, Grimhildiz. Sigifriþuz, eager for a noble wife to legitimize his new wealth and ally with a settled kingdom, agrees.

The plan succeeds through Sigifriþuz's cunning. He uses a nighttime feint and river assault—his "cloak of invisibility"—to take the fort with minimal bloodshed, then lets Gunþiharjaz and his forces march in to claim the victory. Brunihildiz, believing Gunþiharjaz to be the mastermind, agrees to the marriage alliance. Two grand weddings cement the new power bloc on the Rhine.

Years pass. Tensions grow between the established Burgundian nobility and Sigifriþuz’s rough Frankish warriors. At a great feast, Brunihildiz publicly slights Grimhildiz, dismissing her as the wife of a low-born raider. Enraged, Grimhildiz reveals the truth of the victory at Noviomagus in front of the court. The secret is out: Gunþiharjaz is a fraud, and Brunihildiz was won by deception.

The political fallout is catastrophic. The alliance with the Alamanni teeters on the brink of collapse. The Comes Haganaz, seeing the immediate danger, tells his king that Sigifriþuz has become an existential threat. His popularity, his independent power, and now his wife's indiscretion have made him a liability that will lead to the kingdom's destruction. Gunþiharjaz, weak and shamed, reluctantly agrees.

In a moment of supposed reconciliation, a desperate Grimhildiz, seeking to mend the rift, confides in Haganaz. She reveals a secret about her husband: During hunts, he frequently visits a specific woodland stream alone. She believes this information proves her trust. Haganaz sees it as the perfect opportunity.

During a ceremonial hunt, while Sigifriþuz kneels to drink, Haganaz and his men ambush him. With no bodyguards, the great warlord is killed. Haganaz then seizes the "Rhinegold"—the entire movable wealth of the Frankish warband—and secures it for the Burgundian treasury, effectively dissolving the threat.

Part Two: The Roman Eagle and the Vulture's Vengeance

Grimhildiz is left a widow, stripped of her husband, her status, and her fortune. Her grief curdles into a cold, singular desire for vengeance against her brother and, most of all, against Haganaz. Realizing she has no power within the Germanic world, she looks to the only other power in Gaul: Rome.

She journeys to the court of the Magister Militum of Gaul, Flavius Aetius. She presents herself not as a prospective wife, but as a wronged noble and a valuable political tool. She offers Aetius a proposition:
- A casus belli: a legitimate reason to intervene against the Burgundians, who have murdered a powerful leader and seized his wealth, thus destabilizing the Rhine frontier—something Aetius cannot tolerate.
- Intelligence: All the military secrets of her brother's kingdom.
- Treasure: The location of her husband's stolen treasure, to refill Aetius's coffers.

Aetius sees the perfect opportunity. Gunþiharjaz has been getting too independent, expanding his influence beyond the territory allotted to him by treaty. Grimhildiz’s plight is the perfect political cover. He has no interest in her justice, only in the reassertion of Roman dominance.

Aetius accepts her offer. He unleashes the force he keeps on retainer for such brutal work: his Hunnic foederati. They besiege Vangionum (Worms) in the year 437 AD. A massive force of Hunnic cavalry, commanded by Roman officers under the eagle standards of Aetius, descends upon the Burgundian heartland with shocking speed and ferocity.

The Burgundians, caught by surprise, are besieged in their own capital. The final scenes depict the sack of the city. Gunþiharjaz is slain leading a futile defense of his royal hall. Haganaz fights to the last, a grim titan among the flames, falling not to a rival's sword but overwhelmed by a tide of foreign mercenaries.

Grimhildiz watches the burning of her childhood home from Aetius's command tent on a hill overlooking the Rhine. She has her revenge. Her enemies are dead, their kingdom is utterly annihilated, their people scattered or enslaved. But she is now a stateless exile, an instrument whose purpose is served. Aetius does not even grand her exile, instead orders her murder.
